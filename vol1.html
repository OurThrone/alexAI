Foreword · The World of Ashenveil

Before you enter this story, you must understand the land that holds it. Ashenveil is not a great city. It is not a fortress or a seat of power. It is a village — old, stubborn, and quietly afraid. It sits at the meeting point of three forests: the Thornwood to the east, a dense and briar-choked woodland where hunters go and sometimes do not return; the Greymantle to the north, a pale and foggy expanse whose trees grow white and whose birds never sing; and the Deepreach to the west, the oldest forest in all the known lands, older than the kingdoms, older than written memory, so dark and so vast that even the most seasoned rangers mark its edges on their maps and write simply: Here ends the known world.

Ashenveil has one great protection: the ward. A shimmering forcefield of elemental energy, woven over generations by five anchors — five people of power bound to the village by blood and oath. The five elements required are earth, water, air, fire, and shadow. For years, the ward held firm. But the fifth anchor — the shadow anchor — has been missing for decades. The forcefield has been incomplete. And something in the Deepreach has noticed the gap.

Magic in this world is elemental and personal. Every individual with power has a unique relationship with their element — it is not simply a tool but an extension of identity. Shadow magic is the rarest and most feared. Air magic is free and wild. Earth is patient. Water heals and drowns in equal measure. Fire remembers everything it burns. And above all of these, there is the Void — and those who have touched it are never quite the same again.

This is the world into which Nicholas Shadowrider was born. An outcast, a boy with two elements in a village that fears one of them, carrying a pact he did not ask for and a destiny he does not yet know is his.

Prologue

A Watching from the Void

Between the worlds there is a place with no name, no light, no sound. He has lived there long enough that he no longer misses those things.

There was a space between the worlds. It had no name because no language belonging to the living had ever needed to describe it. It existed in the negative — the gap between one reality and the next, the pause between heartbeats, the silence after thunder stops but before the next bolt decides to fall. It was made of the absence of things. And in that absence, something was building.

Dimitreus stood — if standing was the right word for what he did in a place without ground — at the centre of nothing, and he watched. Watching was what he did now. It was patience, and patience was power, and he had learned the shape of power from the inside out by losing it completely and spending two decades clawing it back piece by piece from the bones of the void.

The ward was failing. He could see it the way you could see a candle through a dirty window — the light dimmer than it should be, guttering, the flame bending in a draft that should not be there. The forcefield around Ashenveil had held for sixty years on the strength of four anchors. Four was not enough. Four was never enough.

He turned his attention toward a specific house on a specific lane. Toward a boy who walked with his head down and his shadow slightly wrong. The shadow moved a half-second after the boy did. Most people did not notice that. Dimitreus noticed. Dimitreus looked at everything.

The boy was the fifth anchor. Shadow and air. A warlock pact sealed in the generation before. The boy would either become the thing that saved Ashenveil or the thing that destroyed it. Dimitreus found both outcomes acceptable. He settled deeper into the nothing and continued to watch.

· · ·

In the village of Ashenveil, a boy named Nicholas was walking to the well. He did not know he was being watched. He did not know what was coming. He did not know what he was. Not yet.

I
Chapter One

The Boy the Village Forgot

An outcast is not someone who was rejected. An outcast is someone who was seen clearly — and the ones who saw them were afraid. — From the Codex of Ashenveil, Third Reckoning

Nicholas Shadowrider was seventeen years old and had learned to walk as if he was not quite there. It was a skill that took years to perfect. The trick was in the shoulders — keep them slightly curved, never squared, never claiming space. The eyes stayed down or mid-distance, never meeting. The footfalls were light, deliberate, the pace of someone moving through a room full of sleeping animals. He had started learning it at nine when the first stone was thrown. He had completed it by twelve when the stones stopped and the silence replaced them, which was worse.

Silence was organised. Silence was a decision. A stone thrown in anger was personal — a flash of fear given kinetic shape. But silence was the whole village deciding, collectively and quietly, that Nicholas Shadowrider would not be addressed unless absolutely necessary, and that the gap he walked through when he moved between buildings was not truly a gap but rather a space that belonged to something they preferred not to name.

He understood it. That was the worst part. His shadow was wrong. It fell slightly behind him. It moved in the same direction he moved but with a delay — sometimes half a second, sometimes a full second, long enough that if you watched carefully you would notice it and then feel, at the base of your spine, something cold and quiet that your body identified as danger before your mind had caught up.

· · ·

The well was in the centre square. He came through the narrow lane and found the square occupied. Seven people. Elder Corvin standing near the well as if he had been positioned there. Herath the blacksmith. His wife, Dorn. The Cavins family. Nicholas kept walking. He reached the well. He set his bucket down. He began to lower the rope. Elder Corvin said nothing. The silence was so complete that Nicholas could hear the rope paying out against the old wooden winch.

It was Tessa who made the mistake of looking at his shadow. He heard the small, involuntary sound she made — not quite a word, more like the beginning of one that she stopped.

🧓
Elder Corvin
"The mist was closer last night."

— not to Nicholas, but to the air

🧓
Herath the Blacksmith
"Three fingers of ground past the first marker stones. Checked it myself at dawn."
👤
Dorn
"The ward should hold."
🧓
Elder Corvin
"The ward held better when there were five anchors."

— this time with a direction to it. A slight inclination toward Nicholas that was not quite looking at him and not quite not.

Nicholas filled his bucket. He coiled the well rope back on its hook with the same care he always used. He picked up his bucket. He walked away. He did not let his hands shake until he was back in the lane, and then only slightly, and only for a moment.

· · ·

The house Nicholas shared with his mother was on the far western lane — the lane closest to the Deepreach. Alea was at the long table, working through a set of dried root bundles with a bone-handled knife and a methodical patience that she applied to all physical tasks.

🌑
Nicholas
"The mist was closer."
🌿
Alea
"I felt it. Since before dawn."
🌑
Nicholas
"Elder Corvin was at the well."
🌿
Alea
"Yes."
🌑
Nicholas
"He said the ward held better with five anchors."
🌿
Alea
"He says that often."
🌑
Nicholas
"In my direction."
🌿
Alea
"Nicholas. You were the fifth anchor before you were born. The pact was sealed. You were always going to carry that. I cannot fix what the village makes of it."
🌑
Nicholas
"You could tell them."
🌿
Alea
"I have told them. Your grandmother has told them. Your grandfather has told them twice in formal council. People hear what they are ready to hear."

Nicholas stood at the window and looked west at the dark mass of the Deepreach on the horizon. The mist — thin, grey-blue, low to the ground — crept another inch forward across the scrubland while he watched.

Chapter Two

Smoke at the Edge of Thornwood

The Deepreach does not attack. It simply remembers — and when the things inside it stir, it is because they have remembered something they wish had stayed forgotten. — Ranger Dispatch, Northern Ashenveil Reach, Year 847

The mist came to the marker stones three days later. Not because it had been repelled — the mist had gathered the information it came for. It knew the gap. It knew the shape of the missing anchor. It would come back with that knowledge.

On the eighth day, he went to BunBun. The shop was on the eastern edge of Ashenveil, past the pump-house, on a lane that most people avoided without being able to articulate exactly why. The interior smelled of old wood, metal oil, and something floral that changed slightly on every visit. BunBun was behind the counter. BunBun was always behind the counter.

🏮
BunBun
"Nicholas."
🌑
Nicholas
"I need something."
🏮
BunBun
"You always need something. Sit down."
🌑
Nicholas
"The mist is testing the ward. My family is exhausted holding four points that are supposed to be five. I want to do something about it."
🏮
BunBun
"Do you know what you are?"
🌑
Nicholas
"I am the fifth anchor. I am a warlock — or the beginning of one. I have shadow and air in me. I have a pact I did not sign but that is mine anyway."
🏮
BunBun
"That is the technical answer. Do you know what you are?"
🌑
Nicholas
"No."
🏮
BunBun
"Good. That is the right answer. People who know what they are have stopped looking. You are still looking. That is important."

BunBun reached beneath the counter and produced something small — about the size of a closed fist, made of dark metal with a surface like old water. Shaped like a compass with eight points instead of the usual four, and in place of the magnetic needle, a thin shadow-line that moved, slowly, on its own.

🏮
BunBun
"A shadow compass. It does not point north. It points toward what you need to find."

— The needle was pointing west. Toward the Deepreach.

🌑
Nicholas
"How much?"
🏮
BunBun
"You cannot afford it. I am giving it to you."
🌑
Nicholas
"Why?"
🏮
BunBun
"Because someone gave it to me when I needed it. And because what is coming from the Deepreach will arrive regardless of whether you are ready for it, and I would rather you have a tool than not."
🌑
Nicholas
"Take care of it. It is not indestructible."
🏮
BunBun
"Neither am I."
🌑
Nicholas
"No. But you are harder to break than you think."
IV
Chapter Four

Powers That Should Not Exist

Shadow is not darkness. Darkness is the absence of light. Shadow is the proof that light exists — and that something stands between the light and what it would illuminate. — From the Warlock Codex, authorship disputed

He practiced at night. The back garden. The stone bench. The apple tree. The shadow came easily when he did not fight it. The air was different — air came with a kind of joy that shadow did not have. When he let both out at once — shadow and air together, not fighting, just coexisting — something happened that was neither element separately. The air grew darker. And the shadow moved faster, carried by the wind, directional rather than ambient. Storm-shadow, he thought. That is what it is.

He practiced until the moon was high and then sat on the bench and breathed. His hands were dark at the fingertips — a shadow-dark that faded slowly as he pulled the power back in.

🌹
Unknown Voice
"You look better when you let it out."

Nicholas was on his feet in an instant, turned, the shadow already rising in his hands before he had thought about reaching for it. Standing at the western edge of the garden was someone he had not heard approach — and had not felt approach, which was wrong on both counts. He felt everyone who came near him. This person had no empathic signal at all.

He was male. Pale as winter stone, with hair so black it had a slight crimson cast where the moonlight caught it. His eyes were grey with a spiral quality. He was wearing dark clothes that were somehow very elegant, and he was holding a wand with silver veining that moved slightly as Nicholas looked at it. And the air smelled of burning roses.

🌑
Nicholas
"Who are you?"
🌹
Prolith
"Someone who has been watching you practice. You have real talent. The shadow-air combination is rare. Most warlocks do not discover it on their own."
🌑
Nicholas
"I did not ask about my power. I asked who you are."
🌹
Prolith
"My name is Prolith. I am not a threat to you."
🌑
Nicholas
"You have no empathic signal. Every person has one. You have none. That is not how people work."
🌹
Prolith
"I am not precisely a person. I am something adjacent to it. A category that does not have a clean name in the current vocabulary."
🌑
Nicholas
"What do you want?"
🌹
Prolith
"Currently? To watch you practice. The warlock pact in your bloodline is unusual — most inherited pacts are dormant by the third generation. Yours is not only active, it is growing. I am curious about why."
🌑
Nicholas
"I am going to go back inside."
🌹
Prolith
"Of course. I will be here tomorrow if you have questions."
🌑
Nicholas
"I will not have questions."
🌹
Prolith
"You will. You have a great many questions. You have been keeping them inside because there is no one to ask. But the questions are there. I can hear them."
🌑
Nicholas
"The scent of burning roses. I will know when you are near."
🌹
Prolith
"I would expect nothing less."

Chapter Eight

Companions Gathering

The right people do not appear because you need them. They appear because the world has been moving them toward you for a long time, and the moment arrives when the geometry is right. — From the Lore of the Wandering Paths

They began arriving the week after the forcefield event. Nicholas did not send for them. They came because the word had moved — through the ranger networks, through the trade roads, through whatever communication system exists among people who know that certain events in certain places are beginning points rather than endings.

Valyren arrived first. He was taller than average, lean in the way that very good fighters were lean — functional, nothing wasted. Twin blades at his back. A length of living chain at his hip that rested there rather than being attached.

⛓️
Valyren
"I heard about the ward. About the fifth anchor going active."
🌑
Nicholas
"Word travels."
⛓️
Valyren
"To people who are listening for it, yes."

— He sat down at the table without being invited. Confident rather than rude.

⛓️
Valyren
"You are going to the Deepreach."
🌑
Nicholas
"Eventually."
⛓️
Valyren
"I am going with you. Because the Earth Tower has been inactive for forty years, and I have been looking for something worth going toward for two years, and this is it. Also because shadow-air warlocks need someone at their left flank."
🌑
Nicholas
"I do not know you."
⛓️
Valyren
"That is going to change."

Nicholas looked at Alexa. Alexa looked at Valyren with those deep grey eyes for a long moment and then walked over and sniffed the back of his hand. Then she returned to her spot by the window. Not disapproval. Not yet approval. Provisional.

🌑
Nicholas
"All right. For now."
⛓️
Valyren
"For now is enough."

Adelaide and Dianne arrived the next day. Dianne carried a basket and smelled of bread and something warm. She looked at the room, looked at the people, looked at Alexa, and said:

🕯️
Dianne
"I am going to make tea. Does anyone object?"

— Nobody objected.

✨
Adelaide
"We always travel together. That is not a requirement for others to accommodate."
🌑
Nicholas
"Together is fine."
🕯️
Dianne
"Good boy."

— Valyren made a sound that was very close to a laugh.

Rominae arrived on a Tuesday and let herself in through the back wall of the kitchen, which did not move or open but through which she stepped as if the wall were a curtain, arriving between one moment and the next. Nicholas had the shadow up before she finished stepping through.

🔮
Rominae
"Dark magic witch. You have already been told about me. Or you will be soon. The timeline is a bit fluid from my perspective."
🌑
Nicholas
"Who are you?"
🔮
Rominae
"Rominae. I am here for the expedition. Is there tea?"
🕯️
Dianne
"There is always tea."

— entirely unbothered

🌑
Nicholas
"Soul-sight. You said you can see what people fear most."
🔮
Rominae
"I have made a choice not to look unless I need to. Some people find it less unsettling when they know someone could see something and is choosing not to."
🌑
Nicholas
"Thank you for telling me."
🔮
Rominae
"You are welcome."

Chapter Nine

Laura and the Sound of War

Strategy is not what you do when things go wrong. Strategy is the structure that makes things go right — the architecture behind the chaos. — From the Battle Compositions of Laura Veil

Laura arrived with an enormous case — genuinely enormous, a hard-sided case on wheels that she navigated through the front door with the ease of someone who had done it in much smaller spaces, tilting it at exactly the right angle to clear the doorframe without scratching it.

🎵
Laura
"Laura Veil. Sound Sovereign, Battle DJ, and your strategy officer. Do not argue with me about the last one. I have assessed the group and you need one and I am the most qualified."
🌑
Nicholas
"I was not going to argue."
🎵
Laura
"Good."

— She was already opening the case.

🎵
Laura
"The emitters are directional. This amplifies power tenfold. I have damaged structural stone at full volume. Do not test it inside."
🌑
Nicholas
"We will not test it inside."

That evening, with the full group gathered in the kitchen, Laura spread a map on the table.

🎵
Laura
"The Earth Tower is approximately seven miles in from the tree line. Based on fragmentary records and stated travel times of previous expeditions — most of which did not reach the tower — I estimate five to seven days of internal travel."
⛓️
Valyren
"Seven miles in the Deepreach is not seven miles on a road."
🎵
Laura
"Correct. The records consistently describe the Deepreach as aware. Not intelligent in the way a person is intelligent. But responsive. It reads the group and it responds. The challenges we face are likely to be specific to us. For formation: Valyren — left flank, front. Yulioh — rear defensive. Adelaide and Dianne — centre and protected. July — environmental awareness. Ksu — pre-battle preparation. Rominae — wild card, her own judgement. Nicholas and Alexa — centre front. Me — wherever the strategy requires."
⛓️
Valyren
"That was the best briefing I have attended."
🎵
Laura
"I know. I give the best briefings. Get some sleep. We leave in four days."

— Alexa, who had been sitting beside Laura's feet since Laura sat down, looked up at her with those grey-depth eyes.

🎨
Ksu
"She likes you."
🎵
Laura
"I know. Animals with good judgment always do."
